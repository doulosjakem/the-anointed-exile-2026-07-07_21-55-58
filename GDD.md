# The Exile King - Core Rules (Draft v0.3)

---

# Design Pillars

- Command over individual heroics.
- Positioning over arithmetic.
- Small disciplined companies defeating larger forces.
- Tactical choices should be difficult; math should be simple.
- Theme first. Mechanics should reinforce the biblical narrative without turning spiritual concepts into game resources.

---

# Company & Command Deck

Each company contributes Command Cards.

Example Company:

David
- 2 David cards

Spearmen ×3
- 3 Spearman cards

Archer ×1
- 1 Archer card

Scout ×1
- 1 Scout card

Total:
7-card Command Deck

The deck represents your command options, not your stamina.

---

# Round Structure

The game is played in Rounds.

Each Round consists of four phases.

---

## 1. Command Phase

All players simultaneously:

1. Draw until they have **4 cards** in hand (if possible).
2. If unable to draw because they have fewer than **2 available cards**, they must Brainstorm instead.
3. Choose **2 cards**.
4. Designate one as the **Initiative Card**.
5. Designate the other as the **Support Card**.
6. Place both face down.

---

## 2. Initiative Phase

All players reveal their Initiative Card.

Turns resolve from **lowest Initiative** to **highest Initiative**.

Tie breakers:

1. Lower Initiative on the Support Card.
2. Remaining ties are resolved however players agree.

---

## 3. Action Phase

On your turn:

Resolve either:

- Top of Initiative Card
- Bottom of Support Card

OR

- Bottom of Initiative Card
- Top of Support Card

The Initiative Card always determines initiative.

The Support Card always provides the opposite half.

Unused card halves are ignored.

After resolving:

Place both cards into the Spent Pile unless instructed otherwise.

---

## 4. End Phase

Resolve:

- End-of-round effects
- Persistent abilities
- Scenario effects
- Victory/defeat conditions

Begin the next Round.

---

# Brainstorm

If you begin a Round unable to draw to 4 cards because fewer than **2 cards remain available**, you must Brainstorm instead of participating in the Command Phase.

## Option A — Quick Brainstorm

- Lose **1 random card** permanently.
- Recover every other Spent card to your deck.
- Shuffle.
- Draw up to 4 cards.
- Continue the Round normally.

Represents making quick battlefield decisions under pressure.

---

## Option B — Battle Planning

- Skip this Round.
- Choose **1 card** to Lose permanently.
- Recover every other Spent card.
- Shuffle.
- Draw up to 4 cards.

Represents reorganizing the company and issuing deliberate orders.

(*Playtesting will determine whether both options are needed.*)

---

# Unit Loss

Each unit contributes Command Cards.

When a unit is defeated:

Remove one matching card.

Priority:

1. Spent Pile
2. Draw Deck
3. Hand (last resort)

If multiple eligible cards exist:

Another player randomly selects one.

(For solo play, shuffle eligible cards and remove one at random.)

---

If no matching cards remain:

Lose one additional random card from another unit type.

This represents disruption throughout the company.

---

# Command Philosophy

Cards represent opportunities to issue orders.

More soldiers do NOT automatically create more actions.

Instead they provide:

- Greater board control
- More tactical flexibility
- More resilient formations
- More valuable command cards

---

# Example

Three Spearmen contribute three Spearman cards.

Example card:

TOP

Activate up to **2 Spearmen**:

Attack.

BOTTOM

Activate up to **2 Spearmen**:

Move 2.

This rewards larger formations without allowing unlimited activations.

# Persistent Orders

Some Command Cards remain active.

---

## Spear Wall

TOP

Persistent.

While active:

- Spearmen gain +1 Defense.
- When an enemy enters a space adjacent to a Spearman,
  that Spearman immediately attacks.
- Maximum 3 triggers.

After the third trigger:

Lose this card.

BOTTOM

Activate up to 2 Spearmen.

Move them.

If they finish adjacent:

Activate Spear Wall.

---

## Archer Overwatch

Persistent.

While active:

The first enemy entering line of sight each round may be attacked.

Maximum 2 triggers.

Lose this card.

---

# Reactions

Many units remain dangerous even without being activated.

Examples:

Swordsman
- Riposte against adjacent melee attacks.

Spearman
- Punish enemies entering reach.

Shield Bearer
- Protect adjacent allies.

Archer
- No melee retaliation.

Scout
- Reaction movement or ambush.

Inactive units still influence the battlefield.

---

# Terrain Philosophy

Terrain should create tactical decisions rather than arithmetic.

Examples:

Forest
- Blocks line of sight.
- Increased movement cost.

Hill
- Improved visibility.

River
- Difficult crossing.

Thorn Bushes
- Damage when entered.

Rocky Ground
- Difficult terrain.

Terrain should primarily affect:

- Movement
- Position
- Visibility
- Control of space

Avoid stacking combat modifiers whenever possible.

---

# Combat

Keep combat arithmetic minimal.

Attack Value

+

Command Card Effect

+

Attack Modifier

=

Combat Result

Most tactical depth should come from:

- Positioning
- Timing
- Persistent orders
- Reactions
- Unit synergy

Not modifier stacking.

---

# Shared Attack Modifier Deck

Use one shared Attack Modifier Deck for the entire battle.

The deck represents:

- The uncertainty of battle.
- Momentum shifting between armies.
- The countless unpredictable factors that influence combat.

The deck is shared by all players and factions.

It is not customized or upgraded during the campaign.

---

# Initiative

Every Command Card has an Initiative value (1–99).

Lower numbers:

- Faster.
- Usually smaller effects.

Higher numbers:

- Slower.
- Usually stronger effects.

Choosing your Initiative Card creates a meaningful tradeoff between speed and power every Round.

Future commander abilities may slightly adjust initiative without replacing this system.

---

# David's Identity

David should not simply possess the highest combat values.

Instead he excels at:

- Coordinating allies.
- Inspiring disciplined action.
- Improving positioning.
- Creating opportunities for others.

David wins battles through leadership rather than raw strength.

---

# Overall Design Goal

The player's greatest resource is not damage, health, or numerical bonuses.

It is **command**.

Victory comes from issuing the right orders, at the right moment, to the right people.
